Step 01 of 06
The Location
Every image in your series shares this world. The more precisely you define it, the more consistent your three portraits will feel as a body of work.
Be specific. Not "a café" but "a narrow coffee bar in a European city, early morning, before customers arrive."
Is it dawn, midday, late afternoon, night? Is the light harsh or diffuse, warm or cool, directional or ambient?
A detail that could only exist here — a piece of furniture, a mark on the wall, an object left behind, a view through a window.
What does it feel like to be in this place? Not just what it looks like.
A location is never neutral. What does this one reveal?
Step 02 of 06
Character 1
Know this person well enough that every production decision becomes specific rather than arbitrary. These fields are the difference between a person and a type.
Something visible in a photograph — posture, a feature, how they hold themselves, how they dress.
Something that shapes how they exist in the world and would be readable in a still image.
How do they know each other? What is the nature of that connection?
Not in life generally — in this specific moment.
Every interesting portrait subject is in some kind of tension.

Step 05 of 06
Visual Style
Do not name a filmmaker or photographer. Describe what you actually mean. If you write "cinematic" or "like a movie" you have not answered the question.

The rule: If you cannot describe the visual style in your own words, you do not yet know what image you are making. Every field below should produce language you could hand to a lighting technician or a colourist and have them understand exactly what to do.

Hard or soft? Where is it coming from — side, front, back, above? Warm or cool in colour temperature? Single source or multiple?
What are the dominant tones? Saturated or desaturated? High contrast or compressed? Are there specific colours that recur?
Wide and close to the subject, creating distortion and proximity? Compressed and distant, flattening planes? Somewhere between? What does the focal length do to the relationship between subject and environment?
Is the image clean and sharp? Grainy, film-like, or analogue in texture? Painterly or illustrative? How does the surface quality relate to the subject matter?
Please complete all Visual Style fields before generating prompts.
